How Much Does a Campervan Conversion Cost?

What this guide covers

This is specifically about conversion costs — what you pay to turn a base vehicle into a campervan. For total ownership costs including purchase price, insurance, running costs and depreciation, see the main cost guide.

Full campervan cost guide →

Professional conversion costs

A professional conversion means commissioning a specialist company to design and build the habitation area of your van. This is the most common route for people who want a high-quality result without doing the work themselves.

What you're paying for

Labour — The bulk of the cost. A proper conversion involves carpentry, electrical installation, plumbing, insulation and finishing. Conversion times for a full specification run 4–8 weeks of skilled work.

Materials — Plywood, insulation, upholstery, worktops, hardware, flooring. Material costs on a quality build are substantial — high-grade plywood alone for a full conversion runs £600–£1,000.

Systems — Electrical components (batteries, solar, controller, cabling), water system (pump, tank, fittings), heating (diesel heater or gas). These are significant items in themselves.

Specialist subcontractors — Pop-top roofs are usually supplied and fitted by a specialist (Reimo, Kiravans, SCA and others). Gas installations require a Gas Safe registered engineer.

Typical professional conversion quotes (2025)

Specification Price range
Basic (rock and roll bed, simple kitchen, 12v lighting) £8,000–£13,000
Mid-spec (rock and roll or fixed bed, proper kitchen, 100Ah battery, 200W solar) £13,000–£22,000
Full spec (fixed bed, full kitchen, diesel heating, LiFePO4, 200–400W solar) £22,000–£35,000
High-end (bespoke design, premium materials, full specification) £35,000–£55,000+

These figures are for labour and materials only and do not include the base vehicle or a pop-top roof.

Pop-top roof (supply and fit): £2,800–£5,000 depending on type and supplier

Self-build conversion costs

If you're doing the conversion yourself, your cost is primarily materials. Labour is your time.

Materials breakdown (mid-specification self-build)

Item Budget option Quality option
Insulation (Thinsulate, PIR board, Armaflex) £300 £600
Plywood and timber £400 £800
Flooring £150 £350
Leisure battery (LiFePO4 100Ah) £400 £700
Solar panels (200W) £150 £280
Solar controller (MPPT) £60 £140
DC-DC charger £100 £220
12v wiring, fuse board, connectors £200 £350
Water pump, tank and fittings £150 £280
Gas hob and regulator (if gas) £120 £250
Diesel night heater £250 £900+
Compressor fridge £280 £550
Upholstery and foam £300 £600
Hardware, fastenings, sealant £200 £350
Total materials ~£3,060 ~£6,370

Add a pop-top roof (£2,800–£4,500 fitted) and a rock and roll bed if buying rather than building (£800–£1,800 fitted) and the total rises considerably.

A realistic self-build budget for a genuinely well-specified van: £6,000–£12,000 in materials.

The honest time cost

A first self-build takes most people significantly longer than they expect. Common estimates:

  • Basic conversion: 200–350 hours
  • Mid-spec: 350–550 hours
  • Full spec: 500–800 hours

At a notional value of your time, this changes the self-build economics considerably. The case for self-build is the learning, the customisation and the satisfaction — not necessarily the money saved compared to a quality professional conversion.

Where people overspend

Buying twice — The most expensive mistake in any conversion is specifying a system incorrectly and having to replace it. Buying a 50Ah AGM battery, living with it for a season, discovering it's insufficient, and buying a 150Ah LiFePO4 costs more than buying the LiFePO4 in the first place. Get the spec right at the outset.

Cheap electrical components — Electrical failures in a campervan are miserable and potentially dangerous. Spend properly on your fuse board, cabling and connections. This is not where to cut costs.

Over-specified gas — Many builds include gas when 12v induction or a diesel cooktop would be simpler, safer and require less ongoing maintenance. Gas installations require regular certification and add complexity.

Premium materials in invisible places — Expensive hardwood trim in a van that spends most of its life wet and muddy is hard to justify. Spend on the things that affect your experience: the bed, the mattress, the kitchen worktop, the electrical system.

Where to invest properly

Insulation — The single highest-impact investment in any conversion. Poor insulation means condensation, damp and a cold van. You can't retrofit it easily. Do it properly first time.

Electrical system — LiFePO4 batteries cost more upfront but last significantly longer than AGM and perform better in the cold. On a van you'll use year-round for years, the lifetime cost is lower.

Diesel heater — The difference between a Webasto or Eberspacher and a cheaper alternative is reliability. If you're wild camping in January and the heater fails, the quality differential is not academic. Budget for a quality heater if winter use is part of your plan.

Mattress — You'll sleep on this hundreds of nights. Don't save £50 here.
