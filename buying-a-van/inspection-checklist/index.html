Used Campervan Inspection Checklist

Take this with you

Print this checklist or open it on your phone before you go to view a van. Go through it methodically. If a seller objects to you spending time checking the van properly, that tells you something.

A campervan is a significant purchase. Fifteen minutes of proper inspection is worth considerably more than the price of a second trip, a specialist inspection or — worst case — a repair bill after purchase.

1. Before you arrive

  • Check the registered keeper history on a paid vehicle check (HPI, Cazana or similar). Confirm outstanding finance, write-off history, stolen status, plate changes.
  • Verify the MOT history at gov.uk/check-mot-history. Note any recurring advisories.
  • Ask the seller: who did the conversion, when, and is there any documentation?
  • Ask for photos of the conversion before viewing if you haven't already seen them.

2. The base vehicle — exterior

Work around the van systematically. Don't get distracted by the conversion before you've checked the vehicle.

Bodywork
- [ ] Panel gaps consistent all round (inconsistency suggests accident repair)
- [ ] Paint match consistent — check door edges and shut lines for respray signs
- [ ] Sills and wheel arches for rust (common failure point on older T5s)
- [ ] Underneath: check for excessive corrosion on chassis rails and floor
- [ ] All lights working — front, rear, indicators, fog
- [ ] Windscreen: chips or cracks (these are MOT advisories waiting to happen)
- [ ] Tyres: tread depth even across each tyre, no cracking on sidewalls, age (check DOT date stamp)
- [ ] Spare wheel present and in usable condition

Pop-top or high-roof (if fitted)
- [ ] Canvas in good condition, no tears or holes, seams intact
- [ ] Mechanism operates smoothly — no sticking, no grinding
- [ ] Closes flush with no gaps
- [ ] Struts hold the roof up without assistance
- [ ] Seals visible around the perimeter

3. The base vehicle — under the bonnet

If you're not mechanically confident, bring someone who is, or pay for an independent inspection.

  • [ ] Oil level and condition (dark black = overdue service; grey/milky = coolant contamination)
  • [ ] Coolant level and colour (should be green/blue, not brown)
  • [ ] No visible oil leaks around the engine or gearbox
  • [ ] Drive belts in good condition, no cracking
  • [ ] Battery terminals clean and tight
  • [ ] Service history present and consistent with mileage

4. The base vehicle — on the move

Take it for a drive. A proper one — not just up the road.

  • [ ] Cold start: does it start easily and idle smoothly?
  • [ ] No warning lights on the dashboard
  • [ ] Gearbox: smooth through all gears, no crunch or hesitation
  • [ ] Clutch: no slipping or juddering
  • [ ] Brakes: progressive and even, no pulling to one side
  • [ ] Steering: no vibration or pulling
  • [ ] No unusual noises under acceleration, braking or over bumps
  • [ ] Turbo spools up cleanly (T5 and T6 are turbodiesels — a slight whistle is normal, a loud whine or lag is not)

5. The conversion — general quality

  • [ ] All joinery secure, no movement or rattling
  • [ ] Surfaces consistent — no gaps between units and walls larger than 5mm
  • [ ] No visible water ingress marks (tide marks, staining, soft wood)
  • [ ] All doors and drawers open and close smoothly
  • [ ] Worktop material in good condition — no swelling, delamination or water damage at edges
  • [ ] Overhead lockers secure and closing properly
  • [ ] Floor covering in good condition, no lifting or damp underneath

Smell test — open the van, spend a minute, and notice. A musty or damp smell is a red flag. A diesel smell inside (other than from a recent heater run) is worth investigating.

6. Electrical system

  • [ ] Leisure battery fitted (ask what type and capacity — AGM or LiFePO4, 100Ah or larger is standard for a proper spec)
  • [ ] Battery age — AGM batteries have a 3–5 year lifespan, LiFePO4 much longer
  • [ ] All 12v sockets and USB ports working
  • [ ] Lights all functioning
  • [ ] Solar panels (if fitted): check for cracks, check for output reading on controller
  • [ ] 240v hookup (if fitted): test with campsite cable if possible, or ask for a demonstration
  • [ ] Inverter (if fitted): confirm it powers 240v loads
  • [ ] Wiring: check for any exposed cables, non-standard connections or signs of DIY electrical work that looks unsafe
  • [ ] Split charge or DC-DC charger fitted — confirm it works by starting the engine and watching battery voltage

Red flags: fuses taped over, wire nuts instead of proper connectors, multiple leads running from the vehicle battery rather than through a proper split charge system.

7. Water system

  • [ ] Fresh water tank location and capacity (20–40L is typical for a basic spec)
  • [ ] Water pump working — fill the tank and test the tap
  • [ ] No water leaks around fittings, pump or tank connections
  • [ ] Drain tap accessible and working
  • [ ] If a sink is fitted: waste pipe drains properly (some conversions drain onto the ground — check this is acceptable for your use)
  • [ ] If a shower is fitted: test it fully

8. Heating

  • [ ] Night heater present (if specified) — test it
  • [ ] Webasto or Eberspacher: listen for clean operation, no smoke smell once warm, fuel pickup from van tank confirmed
  • [ ] Non-branded diesel heaters: test fully, confirm fuel supply, check for any error codes on controller
  • [ ] Heater ducting secure and routing sensible
  • [ ] CO detector fitted and working (test the alarm)
  • [ ] Smoke detector fitted
  • [ ] Gas system (if fitted): check hose condition, connections, regulator date stamp, that gas locker vents to outside, that any gas appliances light cleanly

Important: if a gas hob or fridge is fitted, ask when the installation was last checked. Gas in a vehicle is a specialist area — an unsound installation is genuinely dangerous.

9. Sleeping

  • [ ] Bed lowers or folds flat easily
  • [ ] Sleeping surface comfortable and long enough (measure if you're tall)
  • [ ] Mattress or sleeping platform in good condition — check for damp underneath
  • [ ] Rock and roll bed: mechanism smooth, all catches working, back locks in upright position securely
  • [ ] Fixed bed: check storage access underneath

10. Seating and safety

  • [ ] Rear seats (if fitted) have functioning seatbelts
  • [ ] Seat fixings are bolted to the vehicle floor or a proper load-rated mounting system — not just to the conversion floor
  • [ ] Fire extinguisher present and within service date
  • [ ] First aid kit present
  • [ ] Roof ventilation fan (if fitted) working

11. Documents to request

  • [ ] V5C (logbook) — check registered keeper details match the seller
  • [ ] Full service history
  • [ ] Conversion documentation (spec sheet, receipt, conversion company contact)
  • [ ] Any warranty documentation (conversion company or specialist equipment)
  • [ ] Receipt for significant recent work (roof, bed, electrical, etc.)
  • [ ] MOT certificate

After the inspection

If everything checks out, consider a specialist pre-purchase inspection — an independent VW specialist can put the vehicle on a ramp and check items that aren't visible during a viewing. Many will do this for £100–£150 and it's money well spent on a purchase of this size.
