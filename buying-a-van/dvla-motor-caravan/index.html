DVLA Motor Caravan Classification UK

What is motor caravan classification and why does it matter?

When you convert a panel van into a campervan, it's still registered with the DVLA as a panel van — unless you apply to have the body type changed to "motor caravan." This matters for several reasons.

Road tax — A motor caravan is taxed at the private light goods rate rather than the private car rate. For a T6 in a higher emissions band, this can mean a meaningful saving.

Insurance — Specialist campervan insurance policies require the vehicle to be correctly classified. Some policies won't pay out on claims if the vehicle is insured as a motorhome but classified as a goods vehicle on the V5C.

European travel — Some European countries have different regulations for motor caravans versus goods vehicles. Having the correct classification avoids potential issues at borders and with local regulations.

Campsite access — Some campsites restrict access to classified motor caravans. This is less common but worth knowing.

What qualifies as a motor caravan?

The DVLA's requirements for motor caravan classification are based on the vehicle having permanent, integral fixtures for living accommodation. The DVLA looks for evidence of:

  • A fixed sleeping area
  • A cooking facility
  • Storage facilities

The emphasis is on permanent and integral. A camp bed in the back of a van doesn't qualify. Proper conversion fixtures fitted to the vehicle do.

How to apply

The process is straightforward but requires some documentation.

Step 1 — Prepare your evidence

Gather photographs showing the conversion clearly. The DVLA wants to see the sleeping area, cooking facilities and storage. Include photos from multiple angles showing that the fixtures are permanently fitted.

If you have a conversion specification document or receipt from a conversion company, include that too. It's not mandatory but it supports your application.

Step 2 — Complete form V5C

You need to update your V5C (logbook) to reflect the new body type. Section 7 of the V5C covers the body type — you're changing this from "panel van" or "goods" to "motor caravan."

Send the V5C with the updated section 7, your photographs, and a covering letter explaining the conversion to:

DVLA
Swansea
SA99 1BA

Step 3 — Wait

Processing times vary. Typically 4–8 weeks. The DVLA may ask for additional evidence or clarification — respond promptly to avoid delays.

Step 4 — Receive updated V5C

The DVLA will issue a new V5C showing the body type as "motor caravan." Keep this safe.

Common issues

Insufficient evidence — The most common reason for rejection. Send clear, well-lit photos from multiple angles showing all the relevant fixtures.

Temporary fixtures — If your conversion isn't permanently fitted (i.e. can be removed without significant effort), it may not qualify. Permanent screwed or bonded fixtures are what the DVLA is looking for.

Part-built conversions — You need to complete the conversion before applying. Applying on the basis of what you're planning to do doesn't work.

What happens if you don't apply?

Strictly speaking, nothing happens automatically. You can own and drive a converted van without reclassifying it. But:

  • You'll pay road tax at the goods vehicle rate
  • Your insurance policy may not be technically valid if you're insured as a motorhome but the vehicle is registered otherwise
  • If you ever need to make an insurance claim, this discrepancy could cause problems

It's a straightforward process that takes about an hour of your time and a couple of stamps. It's worth doing.

After reclassification

Update your insurance policy to reflect the new classification. Your insurer will want to know, and it may change your premium. Use this as an opportunity to review whether you have the right specialist campervan insurance in place.
