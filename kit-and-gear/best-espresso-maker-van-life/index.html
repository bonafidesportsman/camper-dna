Best Camping Espresso Makers for Van Life — Tested Off-Grid

This page contains affiliate link placeholders. Links will be added once CamperDNA's Amazon Associates account is live.

There are two kinds of van lifer. Those who are fine with instant coffee, and those for whom the first 20 minutes of a morning involves a ritual that would not look out of place in a specialty coffee shop. This guide is for the second kind.

The good news: you can make genuinely excellent coffee in a campervan. The bad news: a traditional espresso machine is out of the question (too much power, too much space, too fragile). The better news: several alternatives produce results that are — with a bit of practice — indistinguishable from what you'd make at home.

Moka pot (stovetop espresso)

The moka pot is the van lifer's classic and still one of the best options. It works by pushing pressurised hot water through ground coffee using steam pressure, producing a strong, concentrated brew that's not technically espresso but is very much in the same family.

Advantages: Inexpensive (a 2-cup Bialetti Moka Express is around £20), virtually indestructible, produces excellent strong coffee, no moving parts to break, works on any heat source including the van's gas hob. The ritual of it — the hiss, the glug, the smell — is half the appeal.

Disadvantages: Requires ground coffee and some attention (leave it on too long and it burns). Not pressure-adjustable so the output is fixed in style. Not ideal if one person in the van drinks flat whites and the other wants an Americano — the moka pot has one setting.

Best for: Anyone who wants good, strong coffee with minimal faff and minimal spend.

Aeropress

The Aeropress is a manual brewing device that uses air pressure and steep time to produce a concentrated, clean coffee. It's a favourite among specialty coffee obsessives partly because it's so forgiving — you can dial it into any strength and style by adjusting steep time, grind size and the amount of coffee.

Advantages: Produces brilliant, clean coffee. Can replicate espresso-style, long black, or anything in between. Very lightweight and packable (the original; the Aeropress Go is even more compact). Easy to clean — push the puck out, rinse, done. Works with any water temperature so you can make cold brew overnight if you're that way inclined.

Disadvantages: Requires paper filters (or a metal reusable filter — worth buying). One cup at a time, which gets tedious for a group. More fiddly than a moka pot in terms of learning the technique.

Best for: Coffee enthusiasts who care about the output. The gap in quality between a moka pot and an Aeropress is real, and if you care, it's worth it.

Wacaco Minipresso / Nanopresso

These are hand-pump espresso makers that generate real espresso pressure mechanically. Fill the water chamber, pack the coffee basket, pump the plunger, and you get a proper shot with actual crema — without any electricity whatsoever.

Advantages: Genuinely makes espresso (not moka pot concentrate — actual espresso). Very compact. No electricity required. Works with any hot water source. The Nanopresso in particular has a good following among van lifers who want the espresso ritual without any compromise on output.

Disadvantages: More expensive than the Aeropress or moka pot. Requires specific grind size (fine, like espresso). The pumping action takes a bit of getting used to. Only produces one small shot at a time.

Best for: Espresso lovers who aren't willing to compromise. The price is justified if you drink one or two shots a day and care about them.

Instant: a genuine honourable mention

Nescafé Azera Americano and, in particular, the range of specialty instant coffees from brands like Grumpy Mule and Minor Figures oat flat whites have gotten genuinely good. If you're a one-coffee-in-the-morning-when-camping person rather than a three-espresso-before-the-tent-is-down person, there's no shame in a good instant.

The grinder question

Ground coffee goes stale. If you care about the result, a small hand grinder is worth every penny. The Hario Mini Slim, the Timemore C2, and the 1Zpresso K-Plus are all compact enough for van life and will meaningfully improve the output of any of the above brewers. Grind fresh each morning — it takes two minutes and makes a noticeable difference.
