Portable Showers for Van Life — The Honest Guide

This page contains affiliate link placeholders. Links will be added once CamperDNA's Amazon Associates account is live.

A fixed shower in a van is a luxury. It takes up significant space (not just the shower itself — the drainage, the grey water tank, the pump, the boiler), and unless you're in a Sprinter with serious interior room, it's usually a compromise that leaves you with both a small shower and a cramped van.

Most van lifers rely on a combination of solutions: campsite facilities when they're available, and a portable setup for when they're not. Here's what works.

The solar shower bag

The cheapest option and, for summer use, a perfectly functional one. A solar shower bag is essentially a water reservoir with a hose and showerhead. You fill it with water, leave it in direct sun for 2–4 hours, and the water warms up to a usable temperature.

What works: They genuinely get the water warm on a sunny day. Simple, lightweight, no electricity, no moving parts.

What doesn't: The water runs out fast (most bags are 10–20 litres — enough for one quick shower). You need sun. The hanging height means most people end up looping a bag over a door or finding something to hang it from, which is awkward without a dedicated setup.

Best for: Summer use, campsite-adjacent travel, anyone who wants a cheap backup option.

Nemo Helio pressure shower

The Nemo Helio is a foot-pump pressurised shower. You fill the reservoir (11 or 22 litre versions), pump it up with a few presses of the foot pump to create pressure, and the shower runs without any further effort until the pressure drops — then a couple more pumps.

This is a genuinely excellent bit of kit. The pressure makes it feel much more like a real shower than a gravity bag. The 11 litre version is compact and packs flat. You can use water at whatever temperature you've heated it to (a camping kettle topped up with cold water to the right temperature works well). The hose is long enough to use away from the bag, which means you can leave it on the ground or on a table and move around freely.

The 22 litre version is more practical if you're washing your hair or having a proper rinse-off rather than a tactical shower.

Best for: Anyone who wants a portable shower that feels closer to the real thing, particularly for off-grid use.

12V pump shower

A 12V shower draws power from your leisure battery, pumps water from a tank or container through a heating element (in some models), and delivers it through a showerhead. Some van builders install a semi-permanent version inside the van or on the rear door as a tailgate shower.

The advantage over manual options is convenience and consistency — if you have a heated version, you get warm water on demand without having to pre-heat anything. The tailgate shower is a particularly popular setup for surfers and mountain bikers who want a quick rinse after a session.

Installation note: A tailgate-mounted 12V pump shower taps into your leisure battery via a simple fused circuit. The showerhead typically mounts on a bracket on the rear door. Total install time for a confident DIYer is an afternoon.

The gym membership

Unglamorous but worth including. A national gym chain membership (PureGym, Anytime Fitness) costs £20–30 per month and gives you access to shower facilities at hundreds of locations across the UK and sometimes Europe. Many full-time van lifers treat a gym membership as an essential utility — it covers showers, keeps you moving, and solves the problem more reliably than any portable shower can.

What most van lifers actually do

The practical reality: most people use campsite or beach facilities when they're available, keep wet wipes for tactical hygiene, and have a portable pressure shower for genuine off-grid situations. The Nemo Helio or a 12V tailgate setup covers the off-grid case well. A gym membership covers everything else.

Trying to squeeze a full plumbed shower into a space-constrained van build often costs more in lost living space than it's worth. The exceptions are large Sprinter builds with dedicated bathroom rooms, and full-time travellers who spend extended periods truly off-grid.
