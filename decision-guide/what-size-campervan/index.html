What Size Campervan Do I Need?

Why size matters more than people expect

The instinct when buying a campervan is to go bigger. More space equals more comfort, right? Up to a point. Beyond that point, a bigger van means harder parking, higher fuel costs, more restricted campsites, and — in a VW Transporter specifically — a layout that doesn't actually gain you as much as you'd think.

The right size is the one that matches how you'll actually use the van. Here's how to think about it.

Wheelbase: SWB vs LWB

VW Transporters come in two wheelbase options: short wheelbase (SWB) and long wheelbase (LWB). The practical difference in overall vehicle length is around 40cm.

Short wheelbase

External length: approximately 4.9m

Who it suits: couples or solo travellers, weekend users, people who want maximum manoeuvrability, urban dwellers who park on the street.

The trade-off: a fixed bed that's long enough for a 6ft adult typically needs to run across the width of the van on a T6 SWB. This means sleeping across the van (which most people find comfortable) or a well-designed rock and roll bed or fold-flat arrangement. Storage under the bed is very usable.

A standard SWB T6 can comfortably accommodate two adults sleeping, a functional kitchen, a decent storage setup and a 12v electrical system without feeling cramped — if the conversion is well-designed.

Long wheelbase

External length: approximately 5.3m

Who it suits: families with children, taller adults who want a lengthwise fixed bed, people who prioritise interior living space over manoeuvrability.

The trade-off: 40cm of extra length sounds modest but it changes what's possible inside significantly. A fixed bed can run lengthwise (head to toe) rather than across the van. There's more room for a larger kitchen, a separate seating area or a toilet. The LWB is closer to a proper small motorhome in its interior possibilities.

The downsides are real: harder to park in tight spaces, higher road and fuel costs, restricted from some campsites that have length limits, and — because the LWB is less common — a narrower used market.

Roof height: standard vs high-roof vs pop-top

This is often the more consequential decision. The roof determines whether you can stand up inside the van, how you sleep, and what the van looks like.

Standard roof

You can't stand up. At around 1.25–1.3m of interior headroom, you're sitting or crouching. This feels less restrictive than it sounds when you're actually using the van — most van life happens sitting down — but it does limit what's possible in a kitchen and makes getting dressed and moving around more awkward.

Standard roof vans are lower-profile, better on fuel, fit in more car parks, and look less conspicuous (relevant for stealth camping).

High-roof

Around 1.9m of interior headroom. You can stand up fully. This changes the experience significantly — cooking is easier, changing is easier, the van feels more like a small living space than a very large car.

High-roof vehicles are taller and catch more wind on motorways, noticeably affecting fuel economy. They also don't fit in many multi-storey car parks.

Pop-top roof

The most popular option on a converted Transporter. The roof elevates (either front-hinged or rear-hinged) to create standing headroom inside. The upper section can be used for sleeping — either a full sleeping area for children or adults, or additional storage.

When down, the van retains a lower profile. When up, you have full standing room and often additional sleeping capacity. Most pop-tops use a canvas section that's weatherproof but not insulated — relevant for cold nights.

Pop-tops cost £2,500–£4,500 fitted, depending on type and supplier.

For families specifically

If you're travelling with children, size decisions are different.

Two children under 10 can sleep in a pop-top roof space on most standard conversions. Two adults plus two children works well in an LWB with a pop-top — adults in the main sleeping area, children up top.

A family of four in an SWB without a pop-top is genuinely cramped and doesn't work well for overnight stays. Either the LWB or the pop-top (or both) are usually necessary.

Child safety seat points are a separate issue: rear seat belts are a legal requirement for any passenger-carrying configuration. Make sure any van you're assessing has these correctly fitted.

The practical rule

Go as small as you can make work. A smaller van parks everywhere, drives more like a car, fits under barriers and campsite height restrictions, costs less to run and is less intimidating if you're new to driving something of this size. The additional space in a larger van has real benefits — but it also has real costs.

If you're genuinely unsure, hire both sizes for a weekend each before you buy. The difference in daily use is immediately apparent.
