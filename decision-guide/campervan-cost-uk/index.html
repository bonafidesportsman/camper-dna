How Much Does a Campervan Cost?

Why the range is so wide

Search "how much does a campervan cost" and you'll find answers ranging from £8,000 to £120,000. That's not because the question is unanswerable — it's because "campervan" covers an enormous range of vehicles, ages, specifications and build quality.

This guide breaks down the real cost by route and by category. The numbers reflect the UK market in 2025 and are based on current transactions rather than aspirational estimates.

Route 1 — Buying a pre-converted van

Entry level: £15,000–£28,000

At this price point you're looking at older T5s (2010–2015 vintage), high-mileage T6s or non-VW bases. Conversions will be basic — rock and roll bed, a simple kitchen unit, basic 12v system. This is a workable starting point but expect to spend money on updating the electrics, improving insulation or fixing wear and tear in the conversion.

What you're getting for the lower end of this range is primarily the van itself. The conversion is a bonus rather than the point.

Mid range: £28,000–£45,000

This is the sweet spot for most buyers. T6s from 2016 onwards with professional conversions, reasonable mileage, and a specification that covers the essentials properly. A well-specified van in this range will have a proper leisure battery and solar system, decent insulation, a functional kitchen and a bed that doesn't require an engineering degree to convert.

Quality varies significantly at this price point. Use the inspection checklist and take someone who knows what to look for.

High specification: £45,000–£75,000

Professional conversion companies at the top of the market, low-mileage T6.1s, high-specification systems — LiFePO4 batteries, diesel heating, fixed beds, quality joinery. At this level the conversion cost alone is £25,000–£40,000.

This isn't a bracket where you're paying for luxury — you're paying for engineering quality, system reliability and a build that will last. Cheaper conversions cut corners on insulation, electrics and materials. Those corners cost money to fix later.

Route 2 — Donor vehicle plus conversion

Base vehicle: £10,000–£22,000

A T6 in reasonable condition suitable as a donor vehicle — unwanted in its current configuration but sound mechanically. The lower end of this bracket buys a higher-mileage vehicle that's been well maintained; the upper end buys a low-mileage example in better condition. Budget at the higher end if you're commissioning a conversion — you'll be putting a lot of money into the van and you don't want mechanical surprises undermining it.

Donor vehicle buying guide →

Professional conversion: £18,000–£40,000

What a reputable UK conversion company charges. Significant variation exists depending on the company, the specification and current lead times. Get multiple quotes and check the spec sheets carefully — a cheaper quote often means a different spec, not the same spec for less money.

This covers labour, materials, systems and fit-out. It doesn't typically cover pop-top roof installations (those are usually subcontracted) or specialist modifications.

Self-build conversion: £6,000–£15,000 in materials

If you're doing the conversion yourself or with help, your materials cost depends almost entirely on the specification. A basic self-build with budget components is achievable for £4,000–£6,000. A well-specified self-build with quality materials — LiFePO4 battery, quality cabinetry, diesel heater — runs closer to £10,000–£15,000.

Your time has a cost too. A first-time self-build typically takes 500–800 hours. Factor that in honestly.

Running costs

Insurance — Specialist campervan insurance through providers like Comfort, Adrian Flux or Brentacre runs £800–£1,500 per year for a standard risk profile. Standard vehicle insurance rarely covers a campervan adequately — check the policy wording carefully.

Road tax — Motor caravan classification (if correctly applied for) attracts lower road tax. At standard vehicle rates, a 2.0 TDI Transporter pays in the higher brackets. Worth sorting the DVLA classification correctly.

DVLA motor caravan guide →

Fuel — Budget 25–35mpg depending on spec, age and driving style. Motorway cruising on a loaded T6 is closer to 28–32mpg.

Servicing — VW Transporter servicing through an independent VW specialist runs £250–£500 per service. Budget for a service every year or every 10,000 miles.

Unexpected repairs — Budget £500–£1,000 per year as a contingency, more for older or higher-mileage vehicles.

Campsite fees — Highly variable. Wild camping costs nothing (and is legal in Scotland). Club sites run £25–£35 per night. Farm sites, CLs and smaller independents are often £15–£25. Factor this into your annual usage costs.

Total cost of ownership (indicative)

For a mid-range pre-converted T6, used for 20 nights per year over five years:

Item Cost
Purchase £35,000
Insurance (5 years) £6,000
Servicing and maintenance £4,000
Fuel (20 nights × approx. 200 miles) £3,500
Campsite fees (20 nights × £25 average) £2,500
Depreciation (estimated) £8,000
Total 5-year cost £59,000
Cost per night £590

That cost-per-night figure looks alarming. Compare it to a hotel for two (£120–£200 per night) and it doesn't stack up. Compare it to the total cost of holidays that achieve something similar — the access, the flexibility, the outdoor lifestyle — and the calculation is less straightforward. But run the numbers honestly before you commit.
