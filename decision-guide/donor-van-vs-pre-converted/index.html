Donor Van vs Pre-Converted Campervan

The most important decision you'll make

Before van type, layout or systems — this is the fork in the road. Donor vehicle and conversion, or buy pre-built? It's the question with the biggest implications for your budget, timeline and what you end up with.

Neither route is obviously better. They suit different people in different situations. Here's an honest comparison.

Buying pre-converted

You buy a van that's already been converted. Someone else has made all the specification decisions, done the work, and you're buying the result.

The case for it

You can use it immediately. No waiting months for a conversion slot, no living without the van while it's being built. You buy it, you drive it away.

The spec is known. You can inspect the actual thing rather than making decisions based on drawings and conversations. You can sleep in it, cook in it, live in it for a weekend before you commit.

No project management. Commissioning a conversion involves dozens of decisions, a relationship with a conversion company, and the possibility of things taking longer or costing more than planned. Buying pre-built sidesteps all of that.

Financing is easier. Lenders are more comfortable with an existing asset than a conversion project.

The case against it

You get someone else's spec. The layout, the systems, the materials — all chosen by someone else for their priorities, not yours. Fixed bed when you wanted rock and roll. Gas hob when you wanted induction. Pop-top when you wanted high-roof.

Quality is opaque. You can inspect a conversion, but you can't fully know how well it was done. Insulation quality, electrical wiring standards, woodwork hidden behind panels — these things reveal themselves over time.

The good ones sell fast. Well-specified pre-converted vans at fair prices move quickly. Finding the right van takes time and patience.

Conversions are hard to change. A layout that doesn't quite work for you isn't a quick fix. Ripping out and redoing conversion work is expensive.

Donor vehicle and conversion

You buy a base van and either commission a professional conversion, do it yourself, or some combination.

The case for it

You get exactly what you want. Every decision is yours. Layout, bed position, kitchen, systems, materials, colours. The van is built around your actual requirements rather than someone else's.

Quality is controllable. You choose the conversion company (or do the work yourself) and you specify the standard. A well-specified professional conversion outlasts most budget pre-built alternatives.

The van is right. You choose the specific vehicle — the engine, the spec, the age, the mileage, the history — rather than buying whatever comes attached to the conversion.

Self-build is possible. If you have the time, the skills and the patience, doing the conversion yourself significantly reduces the total cost.

The case against it

It takes time. The typical lead time from reputable UK conversion companies is 3–9 months. Add the time to find and purchase the donor vehicle and you could be looking at a year before you're on the road.

You're making decisions on drawings. Unlike buying pre-built, you're committing to a layout you haven't lived in. Get the decisions wrong and they're expensive to unpick.

Project management is your job. Coordinating the donor vehicle purchase, the pop-top if you're having one, the conversion company and any additional specialists requires time and attention.

Cost overruns are possible. Conversion projects can run over. Make sure you have a detailed quote and understand what's included and what isn't.

Who each route suits

Buy pre-converted if:
- You want to be in the van within a few weeks
- You're flexible on specification and can find a van that's close to what you want
- You want to see and test exactly what you're buying
- You don't have the bandwidth to manage a build project

Donor vehicle and conversion if:
- You have specific requirements that are unlikely to be met by the pre-built market
- You have 6–12 months before you need the van
- You're commissioning a high-specification conversion and quality matters more than speed
- You're doing it yourself and have the skills and time

A practical note on the used pre-built market

There's a middle route that gets overlooked: buying an older or lower-spec pre-built conversion and upgrading the systems over time. A solid T5 with a basic conversion at £18,000–£22,000 can be updated with a proper leisure battery, solar and heating setup for £1,500–£2,500 in parts. This route keeps costs manageable while letting you improve the van incrementally as you understand what you actually need.

It's not glamorous, but it's often the most rational starting point for people who aren't certain exactly what they want.
